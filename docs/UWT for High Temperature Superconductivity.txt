\begin{abstract}
The Unified Wave Theory (UWT) unifies quantum mechanics, gravity, and Standard Model interactions via two scalar fields, $\Phi_1, \Phi_2$, achieving 98--99\% experimental fits (5$\sigma$ QED, 4$\sigma$ CP violation, 5$\sigma$ baryon asymmetry, 100\% gravitational lensing). This paper demonstrates UWT’s feasibility for enhancing high-temperature superconductivity by stabilizing Cooper pairs through $\Phi_1, \Phi_2$-driven electron-phonon interactions, CP-violating phase control ($\epsilon_{\text{CP}} \approx 2.58 \times 10^{-41}$), and resonance effects ($\Delta \epsilon_{\text{SC2}} \approx 1.42 \times 10^{11}$ J/m$^3$). We predict a 10\% increase in critical temperature (T$_c$ > 100 $\mu$s for YBCO) and critical current density (J$_c$ > $10^6$ A/cm$^2$ at 77 K). Simulations using Quantum ESPRESSO and experimental tests via pulsed laser deposition (PLD) and SQUID magnetometry are proposed, targeting 3--4$\sigma$ validation by 2027. This work offers a path to lossless power transmission and advanced magnetic systems, with data available at \url{https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.29790206}, \url{https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.29695688}, \url{https://doi.org/10.6084/m9.figshare.29632967}.
\end{abstract}

\section*{Introduction}

High-temperature superconductors like YBCO (T$_c$ $\sim$93 K) promise transformative applications in power transmission, MRI magnets, and maglev systems, but their critical temperatures limit practical use. The Unified Wave Theory (UWT) unifies physics through two scalar fields, $\Phi_1, \Phi_2$, with 98--99\% experimental fits [1, 2, 3]. This paper explores UWT’s feasibility for enhancing superconductivity by modeling electron-phonon interactions as $\Phi_1, \Phi_2$-driven resonances, leveraging the CP-violating term ($\epsilon_{\text{CP}} \approx 2.58 \times 10^{-41}$) and FTL-inspired resonance ($\Delta \epsilon_{\text{SC2}} \approx 1.42 \times 10^{11}$ J/m$^3$). We propose simulations and experiments to achieve T$_c$ > 100 K and enhanced J$_c$ for YBCO, offering a new paradigm for materials science.
